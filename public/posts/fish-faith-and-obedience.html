Fish, Faith and Obedience

By Jennifer Asomie

An article adapted from a sermon by Pastor Taiwo Oyemade titled "God is Calling You (Part 3)" at Onebody Church, Yaba Lagos on Wednesday, 20th March 2024.

The scriptures reveal insights into the dynamics between human initiative and divine intervention through the journey of faith and obedience. In Luke 5:4-5, we see Jesus initiating a miraculous event, prompting Peter to cast his nets again after a fruitless night of fishing.

"When he had finished speaking, he said to Simon, 'Now go out where it is deeper, and let down your nets to catch some fish.' 'Master,' Simon replied, 'we worked hard all last night and didn't catch a thing. But if you say so, I'll let the nets down again.'"

Luke 5:4–5 NLT

Peter's response, encapsulated in the phrase "…But if you say so…" not only reflects his willingness to trust in Jesus' instruction but also his recognition of Jesus' spiritual authority over the universe. By acknowledging Jesus' command, Peter relinquishes control over the outcome, placing his faith in Christ.

This act of faith echoes Romans 10:17, affirming that hearing the word of God engenders faith. Similarly, in Matthew 14:28, we encounter another instance where faith is sparked by hearing the voice of Jesus.

"Then Peter called to him, 'Lord, if it's really you, tell me to come to you, walking on the water.' 'Yes, come,' Jesus said. So Peter went over the side of the boat and walked on the water toward Jesus."

Matthew 14:28–29 NLT

However, in this scenario, it is Peter who takes the initiative, asking the Lord to give him the word to step out of the boat. It is crucial to note that Peter's faith is not self-generated but rooted in the enabling power of God's word, documented in Matthew 14:29. Jesus said "Yes, come…"

The proceeding verses in Matthew 14:30-31 further emphasise the inseparable connection between faith and the word of God. Despite Peter's initial hesitation and subsequent doubt, Jesus extends his hand to rescue him, demonstrating his unfailing willingness to intervene when his disciples falter. This also shows the importance of trusting in God's word even amidst moments of uncertainty and apprehension.

Moreover, Luke 5:8-10 portrays the transformative power of encountering Jesus' miraculous works. Peter's overwhelming response to the miraculous catch of fish leads him to repentance and adoration, marking a pivotal moment in his journey of faith. This was not the case in John 6:26, where the crowds focused solely on the miracles rather than the deeper lessons they conveyed, such as the call to repentance and discipleship.

The miracle in Luke 5 was repeated in John 21:4-7. This time, it culminated in Jesus reinstating Peter after his denial. This underscores the importance of redemption in the face of failure. It serves as a reminder that despite our shortcomings, God's grace abounds, offering us the opportunity for renewal and restoration.

Central to these stories is the awareness that our calling is a privilege and gift from God. It is not a matter of competition or comparison but a divine invitation to embark on a transformative journey of faith and obedience. As exemplified in Peter's story, our calling may require us to embrace new identities and roles, not based on our own merit or qualifications, but on God's sovereign purpose and plan.

In conclusion, the scriptures provide insights into the relationship between human initiative and divine intervention in the journey of faith. Through encounters with Jesus and his word, we are invited to relinquish control, trust in his wisdom and power, and embark on a transformative journey of obedience and discipleship. As we learn to have faith and trust in God, we discover that by strength shall no man prevail.
