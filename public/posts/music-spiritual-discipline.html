Music as a Spiritual Discipline

How the songs we sing on Sunday reshape the way we see Monday. A conversation with our worship team.

Onebody began with music. Before there was a formal church, there was a group of friends writing songs together — trying to find language for what they believed about Jesus. That origin is still visible in how we worship.

Music at Onebody is not a warm-up act for the sermon. It is theological formation happening in real time. The songs you sing repeatedly become the grammar of your inner world. They shape what you reach for when things fall apart.

What our worship team thinks about

We asked our worship team one question: what are you actually trying to do when you lead? The answers clustered around one idea: they want to help people pay attention. To God. To themselves. To the room.

"Worship is not a performance," one team member said. "It is an invitation. We are just trying to create space for something to happen between God and the people in the room."

Sunday to Monday

The real test of worship is not how it felt in the room. It is whether the person who sang drives home differently. Whether they respond to a difficult email with a little more grace. Whether the truth they declared with their mouth begins to migrate into their chest.

That migration is slow. It happens over years of Sunday mornings, not one exceptional service. Which is why we keep showing up, keep singing, keep trusting that the words are doing something even when we cannot feel it.
