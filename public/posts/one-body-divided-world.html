What It Means to Be One Body in a Divided World

The church is not a building, a programme, or a Sunday habit. It is a people — called, shaped, and sent by Jesus to love the city around them. Here is what that looks like for us in Yaba.

There is a temptation to reduce church to the things we can measure — attendance, programmes, facilities. But Jesus never described his church that way. He described it as a body. Many parts. One life. One head.

In a city like Lagos — loud, fast, stratified — that image is both a challenge and a gift. The challenge: the city constantly pushes us toward our own tribe, our own comfort, our own lane. The gift: when a church actually lives as one body, it is visible in a way that no advertisement can replicate.

What unity actually requires

Unity is not uniformity. The body metaphor in 1 Corinthians 12 is explicit about this — an eye cannot become an ear and remain useful. What holds the body together is not sameness, but the same Spirit, the same Lord, the same call.

This means that genuine unity in the church requires us to hold onto each other across differences we would never choose on our own. Socioeconomic differences. Generational differences. Temperamental differences. The kind of differences that the world uses to divide itself.

Onebody's particular call

We are based in Yaba — a neighbourhood that is simultaneously home to some of Nigeria's most educated young people and some of its most overlooked communities. That tension is not a problem to be managed. It is our mission field.

When our congregation gathers on Sunday, it is a small sign of what God is doing: bringing together people who would not otherwise share a room, and giving them a reason to stay. That reason is Jesus.

That is what we mean when we say Onebody. Not a brand. Not a vibe. A conviction that the church, when it is working, looks like the world that is coming — not the one that is passing away.
