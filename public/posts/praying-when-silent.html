Praying When It Does Not Feel Like It Is Working

Honest thoughts on staying in the posture of prayer even when heaven seems quiet.

There are seasons when prayer feels like shouting into an empty room. The words leave your mouth and seem to go nowhere. You wait. Nothing shifts. You pray again. Same silence.

This is one of the most common experiences in the Christian life, and one of the least talked about. We tend to share our testimonies of answered prayer and quietly carry the seasons when the answer did not come, or came too late, or came in a form we did not recognise.

What the silence might be doing

The Psalms are more honest about this than most of our Sunday mornings. "How long, O Lord?" is a recurring question — not from someone with weak faith, but from someone whose faith is still reaching, still expecting, even in the dark.

The silence of God is not the absence of God. This is easier to say than to feel. But there is something in the posture of persistent prayer that does something to the person praying, regardless of the outcome. It keeps us oriented toward God rather than toward our own resources. It is an act of refusal — a refusal to let the silence have the last word.

Staying in it

We are not suggesting that unanswered prayer does not hurt. It does. Deeply. The people in our congregation who have prayed for healing, for reconciliation, for provision, and waited — they are not weak for feeling the weight of that wait.

But we have also watched people stay in the posture of prayer through the hardest seasons of their lives, and come through changed in ways that surprised even them. The practice held them when the feelings could not.

So we keep praying. Not because it always feels like it is working, but because it is how we stay in the conversation.
