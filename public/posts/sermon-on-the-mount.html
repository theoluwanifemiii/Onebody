The Sermon on the Mount Is Still Radical

Two thousand years later, Jesus' teaching on the hillside remains the most countercultural document ever written.

When Jesus sat down on that hillside in Galilee and began to speak, he was not giving motivational advice. He was describing an entirely different kind of world — one already breaking in through his presence — and inviting his listeners to live inside it.

"Blessed are the poor in spirit." "Blessed are the meek." "Love your enemies." These are not sentimental ideals. In their original context, they were explosive. They still are.

Why we domesticate it

The Sermon on the Mount makes us uncomfortable, so we make it manageable. We read it as spiritual poetry rather than practical ethics. We treat the Beatitudes as a personality quiz rather than a description of who inherits the kingdom.

But Jesus was not describing a feeling. He was describing a posture — a way of being in the world that runs completely counter to how power, success, and survival are normally understood.

Reading it in Lagos

Reading Matthew 5–7 in Yaba, Lagos, is a particular experience. "Do not lay up for yourselves treasures on earth" lands differently in a city where economic anxiety is a constant background noise. "Do not worry about what you will eat" is not easy counsel when many people genuinely do not know.

But that is precisely why the Sermon matters here. Not as escapism, but as resistance — a refusal to let scarcity be the final word about human dignity and divine provision.

Jesus preached this sermon to people with very little. That is who he was talking to. That is still who he is talking to.
